Are Rising Home Prices Forcing Californians Out?

This is the third in a series of articles examining issues related to housing affordability.



Only used domestic migration (excluded int'l migration- into and out of the US). Details can be found in this project's Github repository.
Combined different data sources to expand data reach: Most recent 1-yr Census migration estimates where possible, otherwise most recent 5-yr estimates; combined IRS w/ Census.

Questions Explored:
- Are Californians really leaving?
- If so, where are they going?
- If so, what are the primary culprits? (are [relative] CA home prices increasing- esp. compared to states/counties where they moved?)

Analysis Approach: In order to track domestic migration in and out of California, I examined migration data from the Census and IRS spanning 1991-2018.

Summary of Findings: The narrative that California perpetually experiences greater outflow of residents than arrivals from other states is firmly supported by the data, as illustrated in the charts and maps below. Although negative net migration is a consistent trend, it appears to ebb and flow, ranging from about -50,000 to -430,000 migrants annually. Three spikes of out-migration appear during this period, with their peaks occurring in 1994 and 2005, as well as the one currently forming.

In my analysis below, I attempt to distill the factors causing increased out-migration by comparing housing and income data of destination counties in peak years versus non-peak years and identifying relevant patterns.
